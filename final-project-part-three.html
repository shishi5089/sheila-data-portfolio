Final Project Part III: From Home to Here

The Final Data Story

The final Shorthand story is published and publicly accessible here:

From Home to Here: How food tells the story of an African student abroad

Changes Made Since Part II

Part II had the foundation - the five chapter structure, two Datawrapper charts, user research and an early Shorthand draft. Part III is where everything came together.

The biggest change was replacing the three placeholder sketches with real visualizations. Chapter 1 now has a real choropleth map of Africa built in Datawrapper using IIE Open Doors 2025 data, showing where African students in the US come from - with Nigeria, Ghana and Kenya highlighted and a personal annotation marking Kenya as my home country. Chapter 2 retained the horizontal bar chart from Part II comparing grocery prices in the US versus Kenya - refined with updated colors for better accessibility. Chapter 3 now has a designed side by side comparison of Kenyan market vegetables versus Pittsburgh supermarket vegetables, generated using Google Gemini from a detailed original brief. It includes botanical translations for readers unfamiliar with the Kenyan greens. Chapter 4 now has two donut charts - one showing diet at arrival, one showing diet after adaptation - both built in Datawrapper and based on personal dietary recall.

Chapter 5 was significantly revised following Part II feedback. The original call to action spoke only to administrators. The final version first acknowledges what CMU is already doing well - Language Lunches, Celebration of Culture events, Heinz College orientations, the campus food pantry - before making a specific and practical ask. The recommendations now speak to multiple audiences: administrators, faculty, dining services staff and student groups.

The Audience

The primary audience is university administrators responsible for international student support, dining services and campus inclusion. The reader statement from Part I still holds: as a university administrator, I want to understand how African students experience food and cultural disconnection so I can make better decisions about supporting them in their first year.

The user research in Part II revealed something important - two out of four interviewees could not identify the intended audience from the story alone. They assumed it was for international students. This pushed me to address the audience more directly in Chapter 5, naming them explicitly and broadening the ask beyond administrators to anyone with the power to make small changes.

The persona I kept in mind is an administrator who cares about student wellbeing and knows international students face unique challenges - but has not thought specifically about food as a cultural and practical issue. This story gives them a reason to.

The personal voice throughout the story is an intentional audience decision. I am not writing at administrators from a distance. I am inviting them into an experience they have not had. The data supports the argument but the personal story is what makes it land.

Final Design Decisions

The most significant decision was using a dark background template in Shorthand. This started as a practical choice but ended up being the right aesthetic one. The story is honest and reflective rather than light or upbeat - and the visual tone supports that. The Africa map looks particularly striking against the dark background with the colored countries standing out clearly.

Color accessibility was a consistent concern. In-class peer feedback flagged the original blue and green combination as clashing and problematic for colorblind readers. I updated both Datawrapper charts to teal and amber - a combination that is accessible and visually consistent. The donut charts use warm earthy tones for arrival and cooler blues for adaptation, reinforcing the emotional arc through color alone without any extra explanation.

Chapter 3 was the hardest design problem. Datawrapper is not the right tool for comparing food types - it would lose the personality that makes this chapter work emotionally. The Gemini graphic preserved the warmth and specificity of the original sketch while being far more polished. The botanical translations it added - African Nightshade for Managu, Amaranth Greens for Pigweed, Collard Greens for Sukuma Wiki - were an unexpected and genuinely helpful addition for readers unfamiliar with Kenyan vegetables.

The biggest thing I took away from this whole process is that good data visualization is about restraint. The most effective moments in the story are the ones where a single clear finding is shown and given space. The bread annotation on the grocery chart, the "My home country" label on the Africa map, and the "Nearly double the rate" annotation on the food insecurity chart all follow this - they tell the reader what to notice without doing all the thinking for them.

Final Thoughts

What surprised me most was the growth. This project started as a simple personal observation about food and identity and ended as a complete data story with five real visualizations, a published Shorthand piece and a call to action grounded in specific CMU context. I did not expect it to come together the way it did.

The presentation went well. The conversation in class confirmed something I had hoped for - that this story resonates beyond African students or CMU. The experience of arriving somewhere new and finding that even the most basic things feel foreign is something many people connect with, even when the specifics differ.

If I had more time I would have made Chapter 3 interactive - something where the reader could explore each vegetable and learn more about it. I would also have looked for more CMU-specific food insecurity data rather than relying on the broader PMC research.

What I am most proud of is the balance. This story does not exaggerate or dramatize. It is honest about what I experienced, honest about what CMU is already doing, and specific about what more could be done. That balance - gratitude and honest critique at the same time - was the hardest thing to get right and the most important.
